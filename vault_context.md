A14  TST 50%, Efficiency 30%, Consistency 20%
Decision:  Weights: 0.50 / 0.30 / 0.20 summing to 1.00
Assumption:  Total sleep time is the dominant driver of cognitive restoration — it determines how many full sleep cycles complete. Efficiency measures architecture quality within the available time. Consistency is a circadian modifier — it amplifies or degrades the quality of individual nights through its effect on the sleep-wake clock.
Research basis:  Hirshkowitz et al. (2015) — National Sleep Foundation evidence review: TST has the strongest single-factor association with next-day cognitive performance across all adult studies. Phillips et al. (2017, Scientific Reports) — bedtime regularity independently predicts GPA above and beyond TST alone, with a weaker effect size than TST. This evidence ordering directly maps to the 50/30/20 weight hierarchy.
Risk if wrong:  These weights are assumed from research priority, not empirically derived from this dataset. If consistency turns out to be a stronger predictor than efficiency in Indian coaching-center students (who may have forced irregular schedules), the weights should shift to 50/20/30. Ridge regression on Kota pilot data will test this.


Total Sleep Time (TST) Scoring

A15  TST top anchor at 8.0 hours (score = 100)
Decision:  h >= 8.0h -> TST_score = 100
Assumption:  8.0 hours is the center of the 7–9 hour optimal range for cognitive function in adults. V1 used 7.5h, which undervalued the upper end of the optimal range. Students who sleep 8.5 or 9 hours should receive full credit — they are not 'over-sleeping' in a range that has documented cognitive benefits.
Research basis:  Hirshkowitz et al. (2015) — National Sleep Foundation: 7–9 hours for adults aged 18–64. Walker (2017) — specifically documents 8h as the target for full cognitive restoration including complete glymphatic clearance. Multiple meta-analyses confirm a U-shaped mortality and cognition curve with the nadir at 7–8 hours.
Risk if wrong:  If NEET students in India have a shorter optimal TST (South Asian genetic studies suggest some populations may function optimally at 7–7.5h), the 8.0h anchor rewards longer sleepers who may not benefit proportionally. This is the highest-priority single parameter to calibrate in the Kota pilot.


A16  Score = 60 at 6.0 hours (impairment onset threshold)
Decision:  6.0 – 8.0h: TST_score = 60 + (h - 6.0) / 2.0 * 40
Assumption:  6.0 hours is the established threshold where clear cognitive impairment begins. Research consistently shows that below 6h, memory consolidation, executive function, and sustained attention are measurably degraded. A score of 60 at this threshold places a 6h night in the Low-Moderate boundary — functional but suboptimal.
Research basis:  Van Dongen et al. (2003, Sleep) — the landmark chronic sleep restriction study: 6h sleep per night for 14 days produces cognitive deficits equivalent to 24h of total sleep deprivation. Participants were largely unaware of their own impairment — a specific risk for self-directing students. Below 6h, impairment accelerates, directly justifying the steeper scoring curves below this inflection point.
Risk if wrong:  The score of 60 at 6h is a design choice. If 6h sleep in NEET students produces worse functional performance than 60 implies (sitting in the Moderate zone), the inflection score should be lowered to 50.


A17  Score = 35 at 5.0 hours
Decision:  5.0 – 6.0h: TST_score = 35 + (h - 5.0) * 25
Assumption:  5.0 hours is moderately severe sleep deprivation — below the working threshold for complex cognitive tasks like NEET problem-solving. A score of 35 places this firmly in the Low zone, indicating the student should not attempt new learning and should focus only on passive review.
Research basis:  Czeisler et al. (2006) — reducing sleep to 5h for a single night reduces sustained attention performance by 50%. Multiple studies confirm 5h crosses into the zone where complex multi-step reasoning tasks (physics, organic chemistry) are severely impaired. The 35-point score at 5h reflects significant but not catastrophic impairment.
Risk if wrong:  The linear gradient between 5h and 6h assumes cognitive function recovers proportionally with each additional hour. Some research suggests recovery is non-linear, with disproportionate benefit at the first extra hour above a critical threshold.


A18  Hard floor of 0 below 4.0 hours
Decision:  h < 4.0h -> TST_score = 0 (floor)
Assumption:  Below 4 hours represents severe acute sleep deprivation. At this level, the student is physiologically compromised to a degree where any study activity is counterproductive and potentially harmful — incorrect information rehearsal under impaired conditions can strengthen incorrect memory traces.
Research basis:  Harrison & Horne (2000) — 1 night below 4h produces impairments in logical reasoning and innovative thinking equivalent to mild alcohol intoxication. Decision-making is specifically impaired. Lim & Dinges (2010) — below 4h, psychomotor vigilance testing shows near-total failure of sustained attention.
Risk if wrong:  Very few students sleep below 4h voluntarily — this floor is primarily triggered by illness, extreme stress events, or all-night studying. The floor communicates 'rest required' but does not identify why the student slept so little, which limits the intervention specificity.


Sleep Efficiency Scoring

A19  AASM clinical thresholds: 85% = good, 75% = poor
Decision:  eff >= 85% -> 100 | 75-85% -> scaled 50-100 | 65-75% -> scaled 0-50 | <65% -> 0
Assumption:  The American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) has established clinical thresholds based on PSG studies: >=85% is clinically normal, <75% is clinically poor (associated with insomnia diagnostic criteria). These are not arbitrary numbers — they are the same boundaries used in clinical sleep medicine for treatment decisions and in the ISI outcome scale used in this study.
Research basis:  Edinger et al. (2004) — AASM clinical practice guideline for insomnia: sleep efficiency <75% is a criterion for insomnia disorder diagnosis. Morin et al. (2011) — ISI validation: 85% is the cut-off for adequate sleep efficiency. V1's linear formula anchored at 50% efficiency gave a score of 55 to a clinically normal student (85% efficiency). V2 correctly gives them 100.
Risk if wrong:  AASM thresholds are derived from adult Western clinical populations. They may need calibration for young Indian students whose sleep architecture and efficiency patterns may differ systematically.


A20  Score = 50 at 75% efficiency (AASM poor threshold)
Decision:  eff = 0.75 -> Efficiency_score = 50
Assumption:  The AASM 'poor' threshold should map to the midpoint of the scoring range, correctly placing it at the Low-Moderate boundary. A student at 75% efficiency is clinically at the borderline of poor sleep — borderline functioning, not adequate recovery.
Research basis:  Design decision to make the AASM clinical threshold correspond to the score midpoint. There is no direct research mapping efficiency percentages to readiness scores — this is the Learnzy-specific clinical-to-score conversion based on the principle that clinical 'poor' should sit at the score midpoint.
Risk if wrong:  The linear scaling between 75% and 85% assumes uniform cognitive impact across this range. In practice the relationship may be slightly non-linear near the clinical thresholds.


A21  Score = 0 below 65% efficiency
Decision:  eff < 0.65 -> Efficiency_score = 0
Assumption:  Below 65% efficiency, the student is spending more than 35% of time in bed awake. This indicates clinical insomnia, significant stress, or illness. Sleep at this fragmentation level provides minimal cognitive restoration. A floor of 0 for the efficiency component is warranted.
Research basis:  Ohayon et al. (2017) — sleep efficiency below 65% in young adults strongly associated with clinically significant fatigue, mood disturbance, and next-day cognitive impairment. Buysse et al. (1989) — PSQI validation: efficiency <65% is a strong marker of clinically significant sleep disturbance.
Risk if wrong:  High penalty nights with efficiency <65% are rare (less than 3% of nights in the PMC12375003 dataset). The floor prevents these extreme outlier nights from creating catastrophically negative scores that dominate a student's trajectory.


Bedtime Consistency Scoring

A22  Include bedtime consistency as an independent 20% sleep component (new in V2)
Decision:  Consistency_score weighted 20%, derived from std of go2bed times across study period
Assumption:  Circadian rhythm irregularity independently predicts cognitive impairment, mood disorders, and academic performance decline — independent of total sleep time or efficiency. A student who sleeps 7h every night but at different times disrupts circadian biology not captured by TST or efficiency alone.
Research basis:  Phillips et al. (2017, Scientific Reports) — bedtime variability >60 minutes predicts lower GPA in college students independently of sleep duration (the key word: independently). Taillard et al. (2021) — social jetlag independently associated with impaired executive function and higher depression scores after controlling for total sleep time. Wittmann et al. (2006) — social jetlag concept: circadian misalignment is a distinct independent health risk factor.
Risk if wrong:  Consistency is computed as the std of go2bed across the full study period — a fixed value per student. This is a simplification: in the actual product, consistency should be computed on a rolling 7-day window to detect within-study changes in regularity.


A23  Score = 100 at std ≤ 30 min, declines to 0 at std > 120 min
Decision:  std<=30 -> 100 | 30-60 -> 70-100 | 60-90 -> 40-70 | 90-120 -> 0-40 | >120 -> 0
Assumption:  30 minutes represents clinically acceptable bedtime variability where circadian entrainment remains robust. Beyond 60 minutes, circadian disruption becomes measurable. Beyond 120 minutes, effective sleep schedule is absent and circadian dysregulation is severe.
Research basis:  Phillips et al. (2017) — 60-minute threshold is where bedtime variability becomes associated with significant academic performance impact. Wittmann et al. (2006) — social jetlag of 2+ hours (120 minutes) associated with elevated depression and anxiety risk. The 30-minute threshold for full score reflects the ±30-minute circadian entrainment tolerance in chronobiology literature.
Risk if wrong:  The thresholds (30/60/90/120 minutes) were chosen to align with the Phillips and Wittmann research findings, but the linear scoring within each band is an assumption. The actual relationship between bedtime variability and cognitive performance may be non-linear.


A24  Add 1440 minutes to bedtimes before noon to handle midnight-crossing
Decision:  If go2bed time < 12:00 (noon), add 1440 minutes before computing std
Assumption:  Students who sleep after midnight have bedtimes recorded as early-morning times (e.g. 1:30 AM = 01:30). Without correction, a student consistently sleeping between 11:30 PM and 12:30 AM appears to have std = 60 minutes when true variability is only 30 minutes.
Research basis:  Standard handling for circular/periodic data on a 24-hour clock. The noon threshold is conservative — no healthy student goes to bed between noon and 6 PM, so any time before noon is unambiguously an early-morning sleep time after midnight.
Risk if wrong:  The noon cutoff is a heuristic. In edge cases (extreme illness, unusual schedules), times before noon may not be after-midnight bedtimes. This edge case is not relevant for the NEET student population.


Sleep Latency Penalty

A25  Zero penalty for latency ≤ 20 minutes
Decision:  latency <= 20 min -> penalty = 0
Assumption:  Sleep onset latency of 20 minutes or less is clinically normal and represents healthy sleep pressure without hyperarousal. Penalising this would incorrectly reduce scores for students who are simply not exhausted — which is actually healthy.
Research basis:  Ohayon et al. (2004) — normative sleep data: median sleep onset latency in healthy young adults is 14–18 minutes. AASM guidelines: latency <20 minutes is within normal range. Standard sleep medicine reference.
Risk if wrong:  Very short latency (<5 minutes) may indicate excessive sleepiness or sleep deprivation rather than efficient sleep. The formula does not penalise ultra-short latency, potentially missing a 'too tired' signal that indicates severe sleep deprivation.


A26  Latency penalty caps at -20 for latency > 45 minutes
Decision:  20-30 min: scaled to -10 | 30-45 min: scaled to -20 | >45 min: cap at -20
Assumption:  45 minutes is where sleep onset difficulty indicates hyperarousal, anxiety, or rumination rather than normal variation. Beyond this point, the underlying cause (anxiety/stress) is more important than the duration — so the penalty should not continue increasing linearly. The -20 cap prevents latency from dominating the overall sleep score.
Research basis:  Morin et al. (1993) — CBT-I criteria: sleep onset latency >30 minutes on 3+ nights/week is a diagnostic criterion for insomnia. >45 minutes represents a more severe anxiety-driven presentation. The cap of -20 reflects that latency is one signal among several, not the entire picture.
Risk if wrong:  A student with 90-minute onset latency (severe anxiety) receives the same penalty as 50-minute latency. The formula does not distinguish mild from severe insomnia via latency alone. The ISI outcome measure captures this distinction but the Focus Score does not.


WASO Penalty

A27  Zero WASO penalty for ≤ 20 minutes
Decision:  WASO <= 20 min -> penalty = 0
Assumption:  Brief awakenings of up to 20 minutes total are normal sleep architecture. Human sleep cycles include brief lightening between cycles (every 90 minutes). Penalising normal awakenings would incorrectly reduce scores for students with normal sleep cycling.
Research basis:  Ohayon et al. (2004) — normative WASO in healthy young adults is typically 10–20 minutes. WASO <20 minutes is within the normal reference range. Standard sleep medicine reference.
Risk if wrong:  Students who use the bathroom once per night may report 10–15 minutes of WASO. This is behavioural, not a sleep disorder indicator, and is correctly not penalised.


A28  WASO penalty caps at -15 for WASO > 45 minutes
Decision:  20-30 min: scaled to -10 | 30-45 min: scaled to -15 | >45 min: cap at -15
Assumption:  The WASO cap (-15) is smaller than the latency cap (-20) because WASO is partially already captured in the efficiency score — efficiency = TST / time_in_bed, and WASO reduces TST. Double-penalising via a large WASO penalty would overweight sleep fragmentation. The -15 cap acknowledges fragmented sleep as a distinct signal without double-counting.
Research basis:  WASO and efficiency share TST as a common variable — they are not orthogonal signals. Keeping the WASO cap smaller than the latency cap prevents collinearity from creating disproportionate penalties on fragmented-sleep nights.
Risk if wrong:  Students with clinically significant WASO (>60 minutes) from conditions like anxiety, pain, or environmental noise receive the same penalty as students with moderate WASO (45 minutes). The cap loses discrimination power at the severe end of the distribution.
